Compliance and Security Glossary

A comprehensive reference of terms, definitions, and acronyms related to compliance frameworks and security requirements

Glossary

Comprehensive Compliance & Data Privacy Glossary

In Easy-to-Understand Terms

  • Compliance: Doing what's required by law or company policies.
  • Governance: How a company keeps its operations in check and on track.
  • Risk: The chance of facing a problem that might hurt the business.
  • Risk Management: Identifying potential problems, figuring out how likely they are, and planning to stop them.
  • Internal Audit: A self-check to see if everything is working as it should.
  • External Audit: A review done by someone outside the company to ensure everything is in order.
  • Regulatory Compliance: Following the specific rules set by government authorities.
  • Due Diligence: Doing thorough research before making important business decisions.
  • Residual Risk: The risk that remains even after controls are in place.
  • Risk Appetite: How much risk a company is willing to accept.
  • Compliance Program: A company's organized plan to follow laws and regulations.

  • PII (Personally Identifiable Information): Personal info that can reveal who you are.
  • PHI (Protected Health Information): Health records that need extra privacy protection.
  • CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information): Important details that aren't top secret but need safeguarding.
  • PCI Data: Data connected to credit/debit cards that must be securely managed.
  • Encryption: Scrambling information so only those with a key can read it.
  • Security Incident: When something happens that puts data or systems at risk.
  • Data Breach: When private or sensitive information gets into the wrong hands.
  • Metadata: Information about data, like when it was created or who owns it.
  • Master Data Management: Ensuring important business data is consistent across systems.
  • Dark Data: Information collected but not used, which could pose risks or create value.
  • Synthetic Data: Artificial data that mimics real data but doesn't contain actual personal information.
  • Tokenization: Replacing sensitive data with non-sensitive substitutes that can be mapped back to the original.
  • Data Lake/Data Warehouse: Large storage systems for different types of business data.

  • ISO 9001: Guidelines to ensure a company delivers quality products and services.
  • ISO/IEC 27001: A framework to help companies keep their data safe.
  • FedRAMP: Rules for cloud services used by US government agencies.
  • HIPAA: Guidelines to keep health information private and secure.
  • PCI DSS: Rules for safely processing and storing credit card data.
  • GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation): European rules that give people control over their personal data and standardize data protection across Europe.
  • CCPA/CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act/California Privacy Rights Act): California laws giving residents rights over their personal information.
  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework: A set of guidelines to help organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risks.
  • SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley Act): Rules to make sure public companies are honest about their finances.
  • GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act): Rules for how financial institutions handle private customer information.

  • Control: Steps or tools used to prevent problems.
  • Audit: A detailed check to ensure everything is up to standard.
  • Gap Analysis: Finding where your process falls short of the rules.
  • Policy: A written guideline on how things should be done.
  • Standard Operating Procedure (SOP): Step-by-step instructions to do something correctly every time.
  • Compliance Monitoring: Ongoing checks to make sure rules are being followed.
  • Key Performance Indicator (KPI): Measurable values that show how effectively a company is meeting its goals.
  • Key Risk Indicator (KRI): Warning signs that help spot potential problems before they happen.
  • Compliance Dashboard: A visual summary showing how well a company is following rules and regulations.
  • Attestation: A formal statement confirming that something meets requirements.
  • Evidence Collection: Gathering proof that shows compliance requirements are being met.

  • Vulnerability: A flaw that could let bad actors cause trouble.
  • Threat: A possibility or actor that could attack or exploit a system.
  • Incident Response: How a company deals with problems when something goes wrong.
  • Access Control: Rules that decide who gets to see or use certain information.
  • Audit Trail: A history log that tracks changes and access.
  • Authentication: Checking that someone is who they claim to be.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Using two or more verification methods to prove identity.
  • Firewall: A security barrier that controls what data can enter or leave a network.
  • API Security: Protecting the connections that let different software talk to each other.
  • Endpoint Security: Protecting devices like computers and phones that connect to a network.
  • Security Awareness Training: Teaching employees how to recognize and avoid security threats.
  • Phishing: Trick emails or messages designed to steal information or spread malware.
  • SIEM (Security Information and Event Management): Tools that collect and analyze security alerts from across systems.
  • Penetration Testing: Authorized hacking to find security weaknesses before the bad guys do.
  • Security Operations Center (SOC): A team that continuously monitors for and responds to security issues.
  • Zero Trust: A security approach that trusts no one by default, requiring verification from everyone.
  • DLP (Data Loss Prevention): Tools that stop sensitive information from leaving the company.

  • Data Privacy: Protecting personal information and ensuring people know how it's used.
  • Data Controller: The organization that decides how and why personal data is processed.
  • Data Processor: The entity that processes data on behalf of the data controller.
  • Consent: Permission given by someone for their data to be used.
  • Explicit Consent: Clearly given permission, often requiring a check box or signature.
  • Data Subject: The person whose personal data is being collected or processed.
  • Right to be Forgotten: The right to have your personal data erased when it's no longer needed.
  • Data Minimization: Collecting only the personal data necessary to achieve a specific purpose.
  • Privacy by Design: Building privacy protections into products from the start.
  • Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA): A check to figure out how a new project or change might affect people's privacy.
  • Pseudonymization: Replacing private data with fake identifiers to protect identities while still using the data.
  • Anonymization: Removing details that could identify a person so that the data can't be traced back to them.
  • Data Breach Notification: Informing affected individuals and authorities when a data breach occurs.
  • Third-Party Risk: The risk that comes from sharing data with external vendors or partners.
  • DSAR (Data Subject Access Request): When someone asks to see what personal data a company has about them.
  • Cross-Border Data Transfer: Moving personal information from one country to another.
  • Safe Harbor: Legal provisions that protect organizations from penalties if they meet certain conditions.
  • Binding Corporate Rules: Internal rules that allow multinational companies to transfer personal data internationally.
  • Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs): Pre-approved contract terms for legally transferring data between regions.

  • Data Classification: Sorting data based on how sensitive or important it is.
  • Data Lineage: Tracking where data comes from, where it goes, and how it changes.
  • Data Retention: Rules about how long to keep information before deleting it.
  • Data Mapping: Creating a visual inventory of what data you have and where it's stored.
  • Business Continuity Plan: A roadmap for keeping operations running during disruptions.
  • Disaster Recovery Plan: Steps to restore systems and data after a major problem.

  • Qualitative Risk Assessment: Rating risks using descriptions like "high," "medium," or "low."
  • Quantitative Risk Assessment: Measuring risks using numbers, like dollar amounts or percentages.
  • Inherent Risk: The level of risk before any controls or safeguards are applied.
  • Control Effectiveness: How well safety measures work at reducing risk.
  • Risk Matrix: A chart that helps visualize and prioritize different risks.
  • Risk Treatment: How an organization decides to handle identified risks.
  • Third-Party Risk Management: Checking and managing the risks of working with outside vendors.

  • DPO (Data Protection Officer): The person responsible for overseeing data protection strategy.
  • CISO (Chief Information Security Officer): The executive responsible for an organization's information security.
  • CCO (Chief Compliance Officer): The executive who ensures the company follows rules and regulations.
  • Whistleblower: Someone who reports wrongdoing within an organization.
  • Data Steward: A person responsible for maintaining the quality of specific data.

  • Code of Conduct: A document that outlines expected behaviors within an organization.
  • Data Processing Agreement (DPA): A contract between a data controller and processor about how data will be handled.
  • Record of Processing Activities (ROPA): A document tracking what personal data is used and how.
  • Acceptable Use Policy: Rules about how company systems and data should be used.
  • Exception Management: Handling situations where normal rules can't be followed.
  • Evidence Repository: A centralized place to store proof of compliance.

  • Root Cause Analysis: Finding out the original reason a problem occurred.
  • Tabletop Exercise: A practice run of how to respond to an emergency without actually doing it.
  • Breach Notification Timeline: How quickly organizations must report data breaches.
  • Containment Strategy: Immediate actions to limit damage during a security incident.
  • Post-Incident Review: Looking back at what happened to learn and improve for next time.
  • Business Impact Analysis: Identifying what effects a disruption would have on operations.

  • Al Ethics: Guidelines for responsible use of artificial intelligence.
  • Privacy Shield: Rules for transferring data between different countries safely.
  • Data Sovereignty: The idea that data is subject to the laws of the country where it's stored.
  • Vendor Risk Management: Checking and monitoring the security practices of companies you work with.
  • Cloud Compliance: Following rules when storing data and running services on remote servers.
  • IoT Security: Protecting internet-connected devices from security threats.
  • Blockchain Governance: Rules for managing decentralized data systems.
  • Automated Compliance: Using technology to automatically check if rules are being followed.
  • Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs): Tools specifically designed to protect privacy while allowing data use.
  • DevSecOps: Building security into software development from the beginning.
